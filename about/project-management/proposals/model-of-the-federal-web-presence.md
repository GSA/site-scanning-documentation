
- The online presence of the federal government is made up of ...
  - websites
  - social media
  - podcasts
  - what else?
- Let's focus just on websites though.
- They exist on .gov, .mil, and non-gov/mil domains (e.g. .com, .org, .edu).
- Let's focus just on .gov websites for now.
- There are 1340 second-level .gov domains registered by federal agencies.
- There's roughly 10,000 public, live websites, as in subdomains across those.

#### Problem:  I want to be able to conceptualize this web presence in some sort of useful way.  

Idea:  

The .gov presence as a whole is roughly like the DC metro area.

Take the Trinidad neighborhood of DC.  It has a population of about 4k people, similar to a federal agency.  

<img width="164" height="144" alt="Screenshot 2026-05-15 at 3 00 39 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/76ed4f6d-1274-40f5-8628-02c3b3e2779e" />

This is where it gets kind of tough.  One analogy of the domains that an agency operates would be the [Single Member Districts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advisory_Neighborhood_Commission#Single_Member_Districts) (SMDs are the subparts of the Advistory Neighborhood Commissions that make up the smallest, most local government in the District).  There's about 345 of them city wide and Trinidad has 4 of them.  

<img width="288" height="361" alt="2023_ANCs_and_SMDs_Map" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/68e7440d-1077-43db-a65f-ff98e169f99f" /><img width="250" height="240" alt="Screenshot 2026-05-15 at 3 27 18 PM" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/c0b73ccd-0869-48bd-b153-f5a92f13bee5" />

Trinidad has about 1,900 homes and businesses in it.  Imagine each of those as subdomains.  

The complexity and detail of a single subdomain/website could be analogized to about that of a house.  The amount of stuff and detail and of one house feels about like the amount of stuff and detail on a website.  

But maybe that's not the right scale.  There's about 115 blocks in Trinidad.  This works better (indeed, there's about 7-13k blocks in DC, which tracks very well with the number of federal websites).  So, let's instead imagine each of those blocks as a website.  The number of people and amount of stuff is comparable to the number of people involved and the amount of stuff on a website.  

So - thinking about the web presence of the federal government is roughly like thinking about all of the blocks that make up Washington, DC.  

Finally, I would analogize residences as text content and systems/applications as businesses.  Many blocks have only residences.  Some have numerous businesses. Some have a mix.
